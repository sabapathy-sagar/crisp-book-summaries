Business Chemistry

'Practical Magic for Crafting Powerful Work Relationships'

Synopsis:
The book talks about four personality types that we encounter in our worklplace: Pioneers, Guardians, Drivers and Intergrators.
It gives practical tips on leading and creating the best possible work environment for them to thrive.



Positive traits

  • - The Pioneer is an energetic freethinker – full of big ideas
  • - Is bursting with creativity and original ideas
  • - Pioneers are natural optimists who are happiest when flexing their creative muscles, and they also thrive when their coworkers join in and contribute suggestions

Negative traits

  • – Full of big ideas but fuzzy on the details
  • – Pioneers’ disinterest in detail means that those around them are forced to pick up the slack
  • – Pioneers have a bad reputation for suggesting creative solutions then leaving the implementation to others

How to lead a Pioneer?

  • – Pioneers work best in an encouraging atmosphere that celebrates their achievements
  • – If you’re looking to build a great working relationship with a Pioneer, there’s one word you should try to avoid: no
  • – If they come up with a poorly-thought-through idea that they’re brimming with enthusiasm about, you could say something like, “Yes, that sounds interesting, and I’m eager to understand more about how it could work in practice.”

Positive traits

  • – This working style craves stability and structure
  • – The Guardian believes that great companies and good decisions are both based on solid foundations
  • – If you’re looking for someone to stop you from running into trouble, for example, a Guardian is your first port of call
  • – His need for structure and stability means you can count on him to plan things carefully and take steps to mitigate the risks involved
  • – is highly reliable

Negative traits

  • – loves to play it safe
  • – Approach him with a new idea, and he’ll tell you all the reasons why it’s too risky, why it won’t work, and why you’d be better off sticking with the old way of doing things
  • – a stickler for the status quo
  • – Unfortunately, to those around him, the Guardian often seems like a conservative stick-in-the-mud who can’t move with the times
  • – And at the current pace of technological change, moving with the times is highly important for most organizations. For this reason, the Guardian is often the least-appreciated personality type

How to lead a Guardian?

  • – Guardians thrive when given space, time, and clarity
  • – Thanks to their need for structure, Guardians thrive on planning
  • – Does not like ambiguity
  • – Try to give this personality type plenty of information – and time to process it – in advance of decision-making or discussion sessions
  • – Be crystal clear about what you expect of them so that they can make a detailed plan for meeting your demands
  • – Does not like spontaneous meetings and shifting expectations from time to time

Positive traits

  • – Drivers might not be popular, but they get the job done
  • – Has the uncanny ability to get stuff done
  • – When she wants to achieve something, she’ll gather all the available data, analyze it, and swiftly determine the most logical way to proceed
  • – When a driver comes to a team meeting, she’ll ask you for the facts, challenge you on anything she thinks you’ve got wrong, and then tell you what the plan is

Negative traits

  • – While Drivers tend to be highly valued by their teammates, they’re also known as the least enjoyable personality type to work with
  • – The Driver’s analytical and inquisitive approach might be great for problem-solving, but to her coworkers, she can appear cold and blunt
  • – Drivers are the least empathetic of all
  • – Care only about completing tasks to the best of their ability
  • – Instead of building consensus among her teammates, a Driver will strike out alone until the job is done
  • – May not have the best social skills

How to lead a Driver?

  • – Explain to Drivers why they need to be nice, and give them clear targets
  • – Drivers, however, thrive when they’re given clear goals that they can shoot for and then smash
  • – if you work with a Driver, the best thing you can do is explain why you’re asking them to do certain things
  • – When it comes to setting expectations and processes, try to build in an element of competition for Drivers, which helps satisfy their need to win

Positive traits

  • – Integrators are peacemakers who value your friendship, as well as your work
  • – Treats you like a friend rather than just a coworker
  • – Who’s never too busy to listen to how you feel or understand your point of view
  • – Likes mentoring people and being a great team player
  • – Makes people and relationships his priority, meaning that tasks and measurable outcomes are often relegated to second place
  • – If he’s a leader, he’ll make decisions that everyone else is on board with too
  • – He’s a natural collaborator who just wants everyone to get along
  • – Are brilliantly diplomatic and have the uncanny ability to achieve peace and progress in high-stakes situations
  • – Is a social butterfly

Negative traits

  • – But a natural collaborator does not necessarily make a natural leader
  • – When Integrators are placed in leadership positions, for example, many people complain that they’re more concerned with building consensus than with making the right decisions
  • – Is driven by a desire to please everyone
  • – Tries to avoid confrontation and conflicts

How to lead an Integrator?

  • – Working alone is the worst possible scenario for an Integrator
  • – Integrators thrive on building great interpersonal relationships. Without them, they quickly lose their motivation
  • – Take the time to get to know who they are as a person, and try to share a little about yourself in return
  • – Instead of questioning their need to help others, support them instead, and try to find ways to reward them for being so altruistic
